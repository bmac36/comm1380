What is social media? The textbook defines Social Media interactive Internet- and mobile-based tools and applications that allow users to post and exchange information in real time, facilitating connection, collaboration, and creation of user-generated content.

  • Canadians are heavy users of social media
  • Benefits to businesses
    • Spreads awareness and information about products
    • Promotes communication and relationships
    • Provides purchasing venue and feedback on purchases
  • Benefits to consumers
    • Access to product information and products
    • Consumer support

There are 5 main types of social media:

  • Blogs
  • Micro-blogs
  • Podcasts
  • Social networking sites
  • Photo- and video-sharing sites

Social Media

Social Media is a part of everyday life for a lot of people. It’s how they keep in touch with friends and family. It’s also how we can share our ideas or projects with future employers. In addition to the ways you or I use social media, businesses and organizations use social media to build their brands and to connect with prospective customers.

You will be tasked with researching social media later, so it’s important to know about different social media platforms and how business might make use of them.

Because social media is based on content that is generated by users, it allows for businesses to engage potential customers and get feedback from consumers. It also means that social media is always changing and fiercely competitive. It’s very likely that you may be expected to conduct research into new social media platforms in the workforce.

Blogs

Blogs generally provide topical or timely perspectives on topical issues. Organizations can use blogs internally for company news or externally for their customers. An example of a successful blog is huffingtonpost.ca, which is like an internet newspaper that provides news and commentary on a number of topics.

Podcasts

Podcasts are audio files that are “streamed” or downloaded from the Internet. They are often delivered in episodes and are used to educate and entertain. Malcolm Gladwell, the popular author of The Tipping Point, started a blog called Revisionist History, which re-examines something from the past to determine if how it has been remembered as history is accurate.
